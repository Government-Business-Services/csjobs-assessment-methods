v.05.

'Persuasive principles' recommendation concept

25-26 May 2023 tested

Purpose

Providing an alternative method of recommending assessment methods to vacancy holders - more seamlessly compared to the Triage concept - so the recommendation is relevant within the context of the vacancy holder's task and moment of 'decision' making.

The notion of using persuasion principles to encourage the adoption of a sound, robust recommendation is a well-established mechanism across most of the social media, ecommerce or media services that we use everyday. Is there any reason why it wouldn't be just as effective when recommending selection criteria and assessment methods to vacancy holders?

Assumptions

We assume that it would be reasonable to expect the service to make meaningful and viable recommendations after a number of campaign set-up tasks have been completed.

We assume that if we were to make a 'sound' recommendation to a vacancy holder, and provide a robust rationale for it, at that moment vacancy holders might be tipped in favour of agreement.

We assume that Civil Servants (and vacancy holders in particular) don't possess any superpowers that would render them immune from the normal behavioural responses that people often have when persuasion principles are used with recommendations elsewhere.

Hypotheses

  • We believe that, the use of a persuasion tactic as part of a recommendation of selection criteria or assessment methods to a vacancy holder could be effective in 'converting' a recommendation into adoption.
  • We believe that, it will be possible to simulate the vacancy holder participant's suspension of disblief sufficiently to engage an instinctive behavioural response to the persuasion stimulus provided (in a manner than can be observed or explained by the participant).
  • We believe that, whilst the persuasion stimulus hasn't be expertly crafted by a specialist, our use of common 'message patterns' will still be capable of rendering an effect.

Design direction

For the purpose of expedience we:

  • re-used various design components that had already undergone a fair deal of testing during prior iterations to construct a campaign set-up harness for this prototype context.
  • decided to present a scenario to participants where they had already completed much of the campaign set-up task and were about to embark on the assessment methods section.

We attempted to experiment with the following persuasion principles applied to recommendations to assess their impact on vacancy holder's perceptions and behaviours.

  • Social proof: "xx% of other vacancy holders in your situation made the following decision".
  • Competition and advantage: "there are n competing roles on CS jobs right now, if you do x you will achieve y".
  • Overwhelming evidence: "77% of users typically look at a job ad for the first time on a mobile phone, and only spend 11 seconds on the page, if you do x and y you'll increase the chance of attracting the right applicants".

The recommendations were placed at the primary decision points for picking selection criteria and assessment methods.

There were various opt-in / out devices that were considered during the design which weren't possible to build, these are described in the backlog below.

Results

What worked well

  • Whilst not viewed positively by observers, the use of the simple CTA, "I'm not sure - help me choose" on the behaviours selection page was reasonably effective in achieving its purpose: to provide a way to engage those vacancy holders that were looking for support without forcing the recommendation.
  • The use of background shading was effective in sign-posting the inline recommendation 'action' area on the page (however there would need to be an effective alternative avaiable to activate screen readers to this visual call-to-action).
  • The the recommendation intro panel at the top of the page wasn't always effective, particularly on longer pages. However, it's worth emphasising that the mechanism is there and easy to find IF the user is looking for support. Similarly if they don't need it they can ignore it.

What didn't work well

  • The online tests page was created and inserted into the journey structure to expose vacancy holders to the list of tests available before further progressing onto selection criteria and assessment methods - whilst not stated explicitly, the page seemed to jar with participants, perthaps this is due to the previous step where they were invited to select the SP elements they wanted to use?
  • On the strengths page, the recommendation trigger wasn't effective (although most participants didn't express any inate desire to use strengths). This may have been due to the poor layout of the page and lack of content to engage users that were not interested in searching, or may have been due to a lack of interest or understanding. The strengths page should be reviewed again to place greater emphasis on inline elements that can be readily viewed and interacted with in additon to search.
  • On the Experience page some data was presented to the user, "77% of users typically look at a job ad for the first time on a mobile phone, and only spend 11 seconds on the page", whilst it was interesting it was not particularly helpful without any clear suggested action for the user to take.

Decision point

  • If the concept is taken forward a stronger experiment may be required to craft and test the use of persuasive principles more explicitly to determine their value for encouraging adoption.
  • The approach of having a online tests page is likely to be acceptable, however forcing vacancy holders through (the gift shop) didn't work and seemed to be counter-productive. Recommend that we take the online test page forward, but rather than adding it forcibly into the task list, we should find a way to present it alongside the SP elements (given the step is about making choices), as this will be the most empowering experience for users.
  • If a recommendation panel is used, it's important that we take forward the concept of providing the guidance, followed by the rationale. Pure stats on their own are not always useful (a la Experience page).
  • The strengths page should be revisited to readdress the balance of functionality on the page to reduce the reliance on search. Support of Occ Psy needed to find a way of presented curated content on the page, e.g., categories of strengths selection as well as behaviours linking etc.

Backlog

  • From the start: would you like us to provide recommendations for selection criteria and assessment method opt-in / out device.
  • Midway: would to like to continue receiving recommendations for selection criteria and assesment methods (after first few) opt-in / out device.
  • Various points: available at the bottom of the recommendation panel, a checkbox and label, "Don't make any more recommendations for selection criteria and assessment methods" device.

v.04.A.

Job library template selection journey

12-15 May 2023 tested

Background

The job template idea was conceived during Discovery and further developed during Alpha. The template spec would be used to pre-fill various parts of the campaign set-up task list, including selection criteria and assessment methods. Vacancy holders would be offered a template based a few preliminary questions, which would effectively fast track them through the set-up journey.

In terms of selection and assessment, the template would include a pre-defined assessment â€˜packâ€™ (carefully chosen set of selection criteria and assessment methods as recommended by experts). The experience would be further enhanced with guidance provided to vacancy holders in-line, in the context of their task when using the service.

Assumptions

We assumme that vacancy holders would welcome the concept of using templates which contain pre-defined assessment â€˜packsâ€™ because it would provide confidence of getting it right, or maybe simply because they're interested to see how others do it.

We anticipate that trust (trust in the source and their credibility or trust in the service etc) may play a part in how willing users are to embrace template use, and accept the implied recommendations without lots of editing.

We found out during Alpha that vacancy holders would invariably cite their need for control in configuring the campaign as they saw fit - we assume that any template with recommended assessment methods would be no different.

Hypotheses

  • We believe that if we could present a strong, robust basis to the template, vacancy holders would be more likely to 'suspend disbelief' and try it out when presented with it 'cold'.
  • We suspect that vacancy holders would expect to be provided with the details underpinning the template before trying it out - we believe that there will be specific factors influencing the amount and kind of information needed by vacancy holders to 'suspend disbelief'.
  • We believe that if a credible 'trustmark' could be identified and affiliated with the template, this would further encourage a 'suspension of disbelief' by vacancy holders.

Design direction

We decided to re-use various design components that had already undergone a fair deal of testing during Alpha to construct the campaign set-up harness for this prototype.

The components included a textarea with autocomplete to search for limited taxonomy items (e.g., jobtitle), standard select dropdowns (grade and profession) and the task-list pattern provided task structure after vacancy holders pick a template.

For the template selection page, we experimented with various 'trust' devices in the information design to assess their impact on vacancy holder preferences and perceptions:

  • What is the impact of having the template 'made' by a named Department?
  • What is the impact of attributing the template to a Civil Service Profession?
  • What meaning is implied by date information when presented with the template 'details'?
  • What impact does using template 'performance' information have on vacancy holders?
  • If template performance information is shown, how should it appear to the user?

Two versions of the template selection page were designed

Each version used the 'trust' devices differently, for example:

  • Information on the performance of the template
    â€” Original provided statements about the job ad in a prose,
    â€” Alternative cited statistics
  • Number of matching templates available
    â€” Original offered up a single template,
    â€” Alternative had two templates, one associated with a department and the other a profession (both cases displaying their respective logos a la trustmark)

Results

Overall, the job library template prototype performed reasonably well.

What worked well

  • Providing information on template performance proved to be quite significant, however the precise choice of metrics does require careful consideration, for example suggesting that a template was effective because it managed to attract a large number of applicants is quite a devisive idea depending on what that means in your context (i.e, previous attempts have failed to attract much interest).
  • Presenting template performance information using metrics, and in particular use of percentages was seen as compelling.
  • In addition to the template details, a number of participants wanted to see a previous job ad that used the template (I suspect that a preview of the job ad is likely to be a deciding factor).
  • Some participants stated a need to focus on their departmental templates, whilst others seemed intrigued by other departments and said they'd be interested in seeing how they did it.

How important is the vacancy's area of specialisation?

Is it possible that a vacancy holder that works within a particular domain that might be more convinced by a job library template from another gov function or profession that operates within the same domain?

For example, when recruiting for a Data scientist, there's a good chance that there will be common behaviours or strengths that could be used to assess candidates irrespective of the department.

However a Data scientist required to join an Actuarial Science team would probably need Technical skills and Experience of using specific frameworks and mathematical models when doing their job. Or, a Data scientist needed for a team that models epidemiological data to predict how a new virus might spread, would also need to possses a particular piece of domain knowledge?

So, if this premise were true, could the use of 'social proof' help reinforce the credibility of a template offered to a vacancy holder within the realm of their working domain model?

What didn't work well

  • When presenting more than 1 template option to users, it's important that any data, metrics etc shown is common to all templates listed (to allow vacancy holders to compare and decide).
  • Automatically marking items in the task list as 'completed' was problematic. There was a strong expectation that an action performed by the template (i.e., pre-filling parts of the set-up) could not be 'completed' without their involvement, review etc.
  • Upon selection of the template (step 1 below), participants were presented with a partially 'completed' task-list with no single, strong CTA connect to (step 2 below) - it quickly became apparent that many participants found the transition disjointed and disorientating.

Decisions to take forward

  • It's likely that there will be more than 1 template option available for certain, common roles therefore the information and presentation of the list and tools to allow comparison should be further refined and iterated.
  • The content of the 'compelling fact' needs to be identified and expressed with careful consideration to avoid backlash.
  • If this mechanism is taken forward a working group involving experts from relevant teams is likely to be key to success, when picking viable facts and figures.
  • The use of metrics proved to be powerful however the choice of metric needs further analysis and experiement to determine their use.
  • The use of metrics when comparing more than 1 option for template was an important tool to aid choice, therefore using common metrics to each option would be necessary to support decision making.
  • Upon selection of a template, the next step in the journey needs to be more related to the user's action - experiment with more proactive use of CTA to trigger the next action from user more deliberately.
  • Experiment further with alternative nomenclature for the use of tags to convey status information in the task list particularly with respect to actions preformed (in advance) by a template for the user upon selection.

Backlog

  • Experiment with the link between the domain model of the role versus the domain model of the template 'designer' as a mechanism for reinforce credibility of the template.

v.04.B.

'Triage' recommendation concept journey

12-15 May 2023 tested

Background

The so-called 'Triage' recommendation concept is more akin to a diagnostic or risk assessment tool - the service would get the data needed from the vacancy holder up front, through a series of questions, process and then present various recommendations depending on the answers (or profile) provided.

Assumptions

We assume that there are a finite (short!) number of questions that must be answered by the vacancy holder in order make it possible to recommend in a reasonable amount of time.

We assume that vacancy holders will respond to a call-to-action to activate it the service, rather than it becoming activated without any user action.

Like the template assumption above, we suspect that the amount of evidence 'rigour' required by users to 'suspend disbelief' will be dervied from specific factors, which we should aim to shed light on.

Hypotheses

  • We believe that if we could present a strong, robust basis to the recommendation provided, vacancy holders would be more likely to 'suspend disbelief' and reflect and consider the offered guidance (offered relatively 'cold').
  • We suspect that vacancy holders would expect a recommendation to be accompanied with the details, rationale etc before any willingness to give it a go.

Design direction

  • We envisage the service would be relatively passive or reactive in its nature - much in the same way a triage nurse might briefly interview an A&E arrival to assess the problem and determine the right priority for treatment (depending on policy and the desired outcome). But unlike an A&E situation, our service would attempt to diagnose any risks that may impact the recruitment process.
  • Given this, the intro content, scene setting etc will have heavy burden to articulate the proposition on offer to set set context and set vacancy holders expectations.

We experimented with 2 ways of showing the recommendation:

  • What is the impact of varying the visibility of the rationale behind the recommendation? (a la progressive disclosure strategy)
  • What is the impact of leading the recommendation with the rationale BEFORE the guidance vice versa?
  • What impact does using template 'performance' information have on vacancy holders?
  • If template performance information is shown, how should it appear to the user?

We decide to omit the use of 'performance' data, metrics etc related to the recommendation, instead focusing on expressing the rationale a prose form.

Alternative

Results

Overall, the triage concept didn't test as well as the template prototype.

I suspect, our assumptions regarding the question answering stage at the start, with hindsight, was naive - one or two of the questions confused most participants, rendering a few to say, "I have no idea".

The perceived relevance of the questions being asked of the vacancy holder, particularly at the start of the journey, undoubtedly had a negative impact on the next stage when the recommendation was revealed.

Our design concept didn't backup the questions with any rationale for them - indeed the rationale wasn't shown until the recommendation was revealed. We didn't anticipate this reaction, and it's possible that the lack of explanation for the questions may have made vacancy holders feel suspicous or slightly irritated.

The triage concept didn't convey any 'performance' data or metrics to further support the recommendation, which may have been a serious ommission.

Despite, the less than positive start, many participants were philosphical about the recommendations presented.

What worked well

  • On the Original design, the recommendation and rationale given seemed to be clear and well-understood by participants. There was a fair degree of variance on the perceived 'credibility' of the recommendations, some of the participants held quite strong views on the use of tests in the process, which wasn't well received.
  • On the Alternative design, separating the recommedations by stage seemed to help participants understand what we being recommended more effectively, even if they disagreed with it.
  • Various participants implied that, even if they disagreed with the recommendation, they viewed them in the broader context of reflection and review.
  • Some participants asked whether the recommendation would 'follow' them through the campaign set-up process, as a reminder of the recommendation. Whilst observers weren't convinced that this would fuel a u-turn on a decision, the sentiment could be interpreted as a desire to ensure that their decisons are taken consciously (and for their reasons).
  • Some participants (minority) viewed a few of the recommendations as interesting and felt that they might use them during the selection task. Or at a minimum encourage them to think differently during the task of selection.

What didn't work well

  • Obviously, the first triage step with the questions was less than fit for purpose, but the mechanism itself didn't necessarily fail. It's possible, that our initial premise (keeping the questions short to hasten the reveal of the recommendation) may have been miguided.
  • On the Alternative design, hiding the rationale for the recommendation under the click 'reveal' panel performed very poorly indeed, one participant suggested that the bullets should be 'expanded by default'.
  • When an online test was part of a recommendation, some participants asked about seeing the details, suggesting that even if they were interested the information provided enough to allow them to seriously reflect on their use.
  • One participant mentioned that the cost side of online tests wasn't addressed with a recommendation, which would have been a barrier for them anyway.

Decisions to take forward

  • The question of online test cost came up and whilst clearly not at the forefront of most vacancy holder's minds, it will be as an issue at one point sooner or later, and needs to be resolved.
  • The importance of giving the rationale for both the question and at the point of revealing the recommendation is clearly a big deal and should be taken forward as a user need.

Backlog

  • Should this triage concept be taken forward, the proposition, positioning and messaging use for the concept within the overall service would need further consideration.
  • If a triage style of concept is to be explored further we recommend an experiment using a steady, longer triage step, which provides clear raionale for the questions - this configuration might produce a very different reaction by vacancy holders.
  • If taken forward, greater thought should be given to how the reconmedations might be 'activated' by the vacancy holder for use in campaign set-up, should they be sufficiently compelled by the evidence.

v.03.

Consolidation cycle

24-25 April 2023 tested

Purpose

An opportunity to consolidate those aspects of the experience that have been robustly tested in the last two cycles, and attempt to further refine that which is still lacking before embarking on iteration 4 & 5 (which will try to determine how far the service should go in guiding the user, through recommendations and establish whether it should and if so, how this might be acheived).

Assumptions

We assume Abilities are the same as any of the other success profile elements, therefore we should allow vacancy holders to treat them the same as the rest.

At the end of the assessment methods journey, we assume that reviewing the decisions have been made would be more useful if the summary page was framed by stage and method (and then criteria) rather than the other way round.

Hypotheses

  • We believe that if we treated Abilities in the same way as other SP elements which can be used to identify relevant selection criteria, vacancy holders might perceive tests as just another assessment method choice available to test their criteria.
  • We suspect that moving the first summary review check point to appear after the assignment of criteria to stage, would provide vacancy holders with a more profound context for reflection than merely just the criteria.
  • We believe that an alternative presentation and layout for the final summary/review screen that emphasises the vacancy holders most recent decisions (picking methods rather than criteria) might be more effective in supporting them when reviewing and finalising the choices made at each assessment stage.

Design direction

  • Experimented with idea of treating Abilities like skills using textboxes to define criteria followed by stage and methods etc.
  • Development of strengths selection by introducing a search, interactive autocomplete style mechanism.
  • Further refinement of data entry on Experience (and Skills) criteria, allowing users to add additional textboxes (3), 6 in total.
  • Tried out the idea of moving the first summary/review check 1 step later, after the 'stage' selection to see if the additional level of decision-making is more significant for users when they review.
  • Explored an alternative style of presentation and layout for the final summary/review to determine if leading the summary by stage and method (and then criteria) might provide a effective tool for review and reflection.
  • Added a lot more guidance content to further support those vacancy holders that really need it.

Results

Again, users responded positively to the selection of multiple SP elements at the start, followed by the structured step-by-step method of working through each element on a page. This further increased our confidence on the effacy of the design solution.

By comparison, our approach to Abilities performed very poorly indeed. Upon reflection, even if the hypothesis were to be sound, the fact that users struggle to distinguish abilities from skills (and strengths) and the very limited/specific method choice available... back to the drawing board.

What worked well

  • The concept of using search/autocomplete to help users find strengths (a la interactive dictionary!) did seem to land positively, however there were worries about knowing what they should type if they are unfamiliar with the dictionary. The notion of strengths linked to behaviours was mentioned and may provide a credible alternative to search on the page.
  • Pushing the first summary/review check back by one step seemed to be more effective for users during their reflections (compared to immediately after the selection criteria alone).
  • Users found the final summary/review check page to be rather overwhelming (when listed by selection criteria first) - however the alternative proved to be a winner.

What didn't work well

  • We experimented with providing tactical guidance at the top of the behaviours selection page suggesting, 'if you select 3 or more behaviours you can assess with an online test at sift'. The intention of raising awareness around the Judgement test as an alternative means of assessing behaviours at sift confused many users, leading to more questions without any more answers.
  • As in previous tests involving the behaviours page, the descriptive content for each behaviour was considered to be useful however limited without the grade-specific details.
  • Our intervention to improve Abilties failed miserably. Ultimately, if Abilities are limited to the tests we have available, then any attempt to allow abstraction as a means of defining selection criteria will fail if the outcome is always the same (e.g., these are the tests we have!).
  • Some users were confused with how the personal statement would be handled when reviewing sift methods. It wasn't clear to users that a single personal statement (up to 1250 words) would be used to draw together multiple selection criteria (rather than a personal statement for each). When dealing with methods that are avaialble in a single instance, a more effective mechanism is needed to draw the other criteria that might be used.
  • Some users didn't feel they knew enough about the method to be able to select it, citing the need for greater information.

Decisions for next iteration

  • Incorporate grade-specific behaviours rather than generic ones for next iteration.
  • Take the first summary/review check page (including stage selections) forward as a strong candidate for future iterations (and validation).
  • Take the alternative final summary/review check page forward for further validation.

Backlog

  • Further explore the strengths search mechanism to refine the task of building a list, identify a way of showing strengths linked to behaviours (or any other model) to ensure there's an option for non-searchers.
  • Abilities / tests remains an issue so future iterations should experiment with alternative concepts to find a meaningful home for them.
  • Identify a better way of presenting one-to-many style assessment methods (e.g., personal statement) during their selection (e.g., methods first?)
  • Experiment to determine the right place to allow users to mark a specific behaviour as 'lead' - behaviours, stage selection, summary review at end or all?
  • Build a robust taxonomy to encompass the range of sift and interview methods that can be used (and obtain effective descriptive content to aid VH during selection).
  • Explore how progressive disclosure techniques could be used to layer guidance content in a way that can support a vacancy holders with a range of knowledge and experience.

v.02.

Cristina's concept

06-11 April 2023 tested

Assumptions

Vacancy holders have a strong desire to be provided with support and guidance.

Vacancy holders have a need to be able to specify and configure their selection criteria and assessment methods in a detailed, granular way - rather than their choices being rolled up, they want to make small changes to how they plan to assess on an individual basis (e.g., using different tactics when using strengths rather than just 1 for all).

(Some) vacancy holders need to add additional stages into their recruitment plan beyond merely sift and interview stages, and the service should enable that if required.

Overarching hypothesis

We believe that if we provided users with a structured, guided process they would be better equipped to identify the most appropriate selection criteria and assessment methods for their specific role and particular circumstance. After all, this is another job in additon to their day job! So if we step forward with our expertise, we can help them navigate through and reach the right outcome.

Design direction

It could be argued that v.02. adds an additional step into the process by virtue of drawing out an explicit stage for assigning methods at sift and interview - compared to v.01. which encouraged the vacancy holder to review their criteria and assign both the stage and the method in the same step.

However, an alternative point of view could be that the further step at stage provides an additional opportunity to reflect on what is being decided and each stage and why. In the same way, the moment provides an opportunty for vacancy holders to cognitively 'pull through' what they have done and fine tune in light of the bigger picture.

In the same way, enabling vacancy holders to pick multiple success profile elements at the start of the journey to allow them to create all their desired selection criteria in 'one sitting' is part of a broader perspective that emphasises the connectedness of all the facets of their plan.

It's worth mentioning that this feature does introduce new design challenges compared to v.01. Should the chosen success profile elements appear all on the same page or sub-divided by element? For the purpose of this iteration, both were modelled and tested with users as variant A and B.

Results

Overall, step-by-step, progressively building the criteria and methods in the very 'deliberate' way was successful - however despite the structure, some participants still struggled with some of the SP domain language.

What worked well

  • Breaking down each success profile element page by page proved to be easier for users to understand and progress through the task with ease.
  • Stepping through the selection criteria and assigning sift, interview or both stages proved to be particularly effective - some went on to question whether the summary/review page might have been more useful after this step in the process.
  • The granularity at which both stage and assessment methods proved to be a winner. Even if the user would assign all strengths to interivew questions, the option to further configure was important.

What didn't work well

  • Apart from 'behaviours' and 'experience', most users had limited understanding or experience of the other elements and struggled to distinguish between them, the descriptions provided proved to be inadequate for those users that couldn't resolve the difference between the other elements.
  • Listing all the success profile elements they had chosen on one single, very long page, was unsurprisingly challenging for users - most struggled to orientate and re-orientate themselves as they attempted to progress through each element. The anchor links provided at the top to allow jumping to anchor sections on the page were invariably missed by some and those that did notice, didn't seem to reduce their burden significantly.
  • Only providing 3 textboxes to add their 'experience' criteria was perceived as far too limiting, however the principle of delineating the criteria by 1 in each box was regarded as providing greater flexibility when it comes to deciding how and when to assess that particular criterion (rather than all of them being treated in the same way).
  • The experience of assigning assessment methods to criteria at sift and then interview stages which easy from an interaction point of view proved to be inadequate regarding the descriptions of the methods.
  • Allowing users to edit their choices on the summary page was implemented in a rather simplistic way, effectively taking the user back to the start of the journey and making changes until they reach the summary page again. This is understood to be a poor experience and isn't in the spirit of the Check answers pattern. This was a compromise to allow greater focus on riskier assumptions in the journey.
  • A significant gap in the journey and experience was the ability to preview the job advert (the summary page was good but the preview seemed to be more important and definitive).

Decisions for next iteration

  • Take forward the multi-element selection start.
  • Take forward the multi-page element pages for adding selection criteria.
  • Experiment with moving summary/review step to step when the user has assigned stage to their criteria to determine if it's more useful for review and reflection on their decisions so far.
  • Experiment with how the final summary/review would be structured - i.e., culmination of decisions made, what is an appropriate depiction?
  • Experiment with a more dynamic method of adding and removing entries when specifying Experience criteria (e.g., add/remove textboxes) to provide users with greater autonomy and control.
  • Explore how we might be able to provide better / more support for those vacancy holders that really need it.

Backlog

  • Document a more direct method of editing the choices made when on summary/review a la check answers.
  • Allow users to easily preview the emerging job advert (especially at the end of the journey when summary/review is carried out) (feels like a QA check!).
  • Experiment with more proactive means of supporting users with more guidance and explicit recommendations.

v.01.

Mark's model

24-27 March 2023 tested

Assumptions

The gap between the selection of criteria and methods in the most-recent campaign-set-up prototype is too wide, and likely to make it impossible for users to maintain the context of their task.

Vacancy holders, particularly those with less experience of recruitment, want the service to be more supportive during campaign set-up. When a VH doesn't know, or isn't sure, of what to do they expect the service to come forward and guide them to the right outcome.

Hypotheses

  • We believe that if we provide a way for users to select their criteria, method of assessment and when they will assess (sift vs. interview) in a more unified way this will improve their ease of use and increase their understanding of success profiles.
  • We believe that if we support users to think about what criteria they need to assess their role before they select of methods, we expect that users will pick the success profile components that are most fit for the role (and therefore the most relevant candidate).
  • We believe that we can reduce the risk of overselection when selecting SP elements by framing them as criteria which need to be assessed for the given role.

Design direction

  • Success profiles should be used, but we need to find a way of demystifying them (via good UX design) so that users know what they (eg Behaviours) do when making assessment design decisions.
  • The thing that's being assessed, and how it's being assessed, should be captured within the same transaction/journey. Creating space between these steps adds to confusion.
  • HMRC have created an 'add to list' design pattern, that should be tested as it seemingly will allow for multiple assessment items to be added via an intuitive UX.
  • To support with getting the best result (ie selection of appropriate assessment items), we anticipate a multi-page transaction when adding an assessment item.

Results

Overall, bringing the task of picking selection criteria and assigning methods/stage closer together into a unified journey did prove to be very effective and seemed to encourage greater immersion in the success profiles. Despite this, the absence of supporting instructions, guidance did seem to thwart some participants nonetheless. The use of the 'add-another' pattern did work well and allowed users to build a list of criteria, methods organised by stage efficiently.

What worked well

  • The use of descriptive, supporting content both informational and as guidance did prove to be effective in supporting users somewhat with their task.
  • The use of a summary page to allow users to review, reflect and edit their selection was particularly well recevied by most users, a number of participants cited the summary provided a moment to take stock and reflect on how their decisions look thus far.
  • The mechanism offered by the 'add-another' pattern was effective as a list-building pattern for the given task and could be readily used by participants.
  • The step-by-step nature of the task-structure and experience was regarded positively.

What didn't work well

  • The information provided on the start page was perceived by some participants as missing key content, which seemed to create more questions than the intention (to act as a primer to the task).
  • When picking success profile elements to use as the basis for selection criteria, it was clear that users expected to make a grand selection up front rather than piecemeal.
  • When presenting methods to users, it was clear that without any descriptive content only the most common methods were recognised by some.
  • Abilities and specifically on the use of online tests, it was clear that there were varying degrees of understanding and experience of their use and the minimal information provided did not prove to be particularly useful for the task at hand.
  • Most of the abilities/online tests were deemed to be unrelated to the role they were looking for and provided little reason for further exploration (e.g., recruiting for a technical role).
  • When selecting methods for a group of criteria (e.g., 3-4 behaviours or strengths) expressed a need for flexibility, allowing them to use different methods with different criteria from a common element.

Decisions for next iteration

  • Take forward the user need to pick multiple success profile elements at the start from which they would craft the selection criteria (rather than piecemeal).
  • Take forward the use of summary pages to punctuate the journey, encourage reflection, edit, refine.
  • Experiment with alternatives to better incorporate abilities and online tests more effectively into the journey and experience.
  • Experiment with the provision of greater inline contextual guidance and descriptive content to better support the presentation of methods to users.

Background & vision

Whichever way you look at it, a vacancy holder's choice of selection criteria and assessment methods is indelibly connected with all aspects of Civil Service recruitment, end to end and front to back.

Premise

Inexperienced vacancy holders or those with limited support often struggle when setting up their recruitment campaign, particularly when using the Success profiles framework to identify appropriate selection criteria.

Some recruitment campaigns run into problems at sift. For example, the panel might become overwhelmed by the number of applications received, which may result in delays to the schedule, whch in turn may lead to candidate attrition. Would the outcome be the same if different choices were made during campaign set-up?

Some recruitment campaigns fail to attract any applicants for a role (and appoint) because the investment required of the candidate when viewed in the hard light of day, simply isn't compelling enough compared to amount of assessment they would have to endure ('over-assessment').

Overarching hypothesis

We believe that the challenges faced by vacancy holders at the start of the recruitment process directly contribute to (at least) some of the poor outcomes faced by candidates.

Given this, we believe that if we were able to improve the campaign set-up process, experience and guidance provided to vacancy holders they would better equipped to:

  • create more effective job adverts, and
  • identify the right selection criteria and assessment methods to evauate candidates properly and fairly (both in terms of the criteria fit to job role and 'fitting' level of assessment).

We expect that this action would create better outcomes for both hirers and candidates alike.

Assumptions

  1. Offering a more proactive service could help vacancy holders by:
    • making recommendations on which selection criteria and assessment methods to choose
    • offering pre-prepared templates, with carefully selected assessment methods, and
    • providing guidance in-line, in context when itâ€™s likely to be needed

We believe that less experienced vacancy holders would welcome the concept of receiving recommendations, using templates and assessment â€˜packsâ€™ because it would overcome an intellectual barrier for some, and for others it would provide the confidence and reassurance of getting it right, first time.
  3. Whilst more experienced vacancy holders may be less compelled by recommendations, a service that provides a 'fast-track' through campaign set-up might be regarded differently.
  4. The best way to provide guidance or recommendations to vacancy holders in-line, in the context of their task when itâ€™s likely to be needed.
  5. The demand for online tests in Civil Service recruitment is expected to increase in the future.
  6. The current suite of available online tests are fit for purpose and capable of supporting the selection and assessment needs of vacancy holders.
  7. We assume that the approval process stage (before a vacancy holder will set-up their campaign) does not provide a definitive and iron-cast specification for the selection criteria and assessment methods to be used when assessing applicants.
  8. We assume that the approval process and outcome does not scrutinise the choice of selection criteria, assessment methods and specifically the manner in which they are to be communicated to prospective candidates (otherwise surely, the quality of job ads would be better, right??)
